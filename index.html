Data source 2: OIG LEIE Done
2

OIG Exclusion List (LEIE)

List of Excluded Individuals/Entities · HHS Office of Inspector General
Free · Public

Every individual or entity excluded from participating in federal healthcare programs (Medicare, Medicaid, CHIP). If a provider is on this list, any entity that pays them for federally reimbursable services faces civil monetary penalties.

AccessFormatRefreshCost
exclusions.oig.hhs.govCSV bulk downloadMonthly full + supplementFree
What we found

The file has 82,749 banned providers. Our job is to check whether any provider joining our marketplace appears on this list. The problem: each provider on our marketplace has an NPI (their unique ID number), but only 10% of LEIE records have an NPI. The other 90% are from before 2007 when the NPI system started.

What those old records DO have: the person's last name, first name, and date of birth. "John Smith" alone would match too many people. But "John Smith born April 15, 1965" is almost certainly one specific person. That's why we built two matching tiers:

Tier 1
NPI match (high confidence): exact NPI lookup. Covers 8,306 exclusions (10%). No ambiguity, auto-fail.
Tier 2
Name + date of birth match (medium confidence): catches the 70,543 older exclusions that have no NPI (89%). Near-deterministic, but can miss if a provider changed their name. Flagged for review, not auto-fail.
Combined coverage: 98.9% of individual exclusions on the list. Only 865 records uncoverable (businesses without individual names, or missing DOB).

What this requires from the onboarding form: NPI alone gets Tier 1 only. To run both tiers, the provider onboarding profile must also collect legal first name, legal last name, and date of birth. Without DOB, we can only check against 10% of the exclusion list.

Validation: 10 NPI test cases caught, 5 name+DOB test cases caught, fabricated names correctly passed. Logic confirmed end-to-end.

PECOS — Provider Enrollment, Chain and Ownership System

CMS · Medicare enrollment registry · Not a gate input
Free · Public extract

CMS's enrollment system for providers who bill Medicare. We evaluated PECOS as a potential gate input and decided against it. It's kept as informational context for this step and as a real input for Steps 2 and 5b.

AccessFormatRefreshCost
data.cms.govCSV bulk downloadMonthlyFree
Why we removed it from the gate

The file has 2,957,262 enrollment rows covering 2,542,195 unique providers. This is every provider who bills Medicare in the US. We initially considered using PECOS enrollment as a gate signal.

The problem: absence from PECOS doesn't indicate anything wrong. Plenty of good independent practices don't bill Medicare: concierge, cash-pay, commercial-only. Failing providers on PECOS absence would knock out the exact providers Meroka is trying to serve.

We also can't use the public PECOS extract to detect revocations. It only contains currently enrolled providers, so we can't distinguish "never enrolled" from "was enrolled, got revoked" without historical snapshots or the separate CMS revocation files.

What we kept: A pecos_enrolled informational flag (does not affect pass/fail), plus a cross-reference check: if a provider is LEIE-excluded but still shows as PECOS-enrolled, that's a data integrity flag suggesting CMS hasn't caught up to OIG's exclusion. PECOS remains a real input for Step 2 (specialty validation, enrollment history) and Step 5b (Medicare billing patterns).

Step 1 Report: Safety Gate

OIG LEIE exclusion check · PECOS evaluated, kept as informational · Completed March 2026
1. What we set out to do

Build the first layer of the safety gate: check whether a provider should be allowed into the Meroka marketplace at all. Binary pass/fail. We started with two freely available federal datasets (LEIE and PECOS) because NPDB requires registration and state boards are a separate workstream. After evaluation, LEIE became the gate driver and PECOS was scoped as informational context only.

2. What we downloaded

OIG LEIE (exclusions.oig.hhs.gov): the federal government's master list of every individual or entity banned from participating in Medicare, Medicaid, and CHIP. 82,749 records. Free, public, updated monthly.

PECOS (data.cms.gov): CMS's enrollment file for every provider who bills Medicare. 2.96 million enrollment rows covering 2.54 million unique providers. Free, public, updated monthly.

3. What we built

A Jupyter notebook that takes a list of provider NPIs, runs them against the LEIE exclusion list, and produces a pass/fail result per provider. PECOS is loaded for informational context but does not affect the gate decision. We tested it on 210 NPIs: 200 clean providers sourced from PECOS and 10 providers we knew were on the exclusion list.

4. First finding: the NPI gap

Only 10.3% of LEIE records have a usable NPI. The other 89.7% are from before 2007 when the NPI system was introduced. Those older records have the provider's name and address but no NPI. If we only match on NPI, we can only check incoming providers against 10% of the banned list. That's too big a gap for a vetting context.

5. The solution: two-tier matching

We looked at what fields the old LEIE records DO have. Every individual record includes last name, first name, and date of birth. Name alone would be too ambiguous ("John Smith" matches too many people). But name + date of birth is near-deterministic. "John Smith born April 15, 1965" is almost certainly one specific person. So we built two matching tiers:

Tier 1
Exact NPI match (high confidence). Covers 8,306 exclusions. No ambiguity. Auto-fail in production.
Tier 2
Last name + first name + DOB match (medium confidence). Covers 70,543 additional exclusions. Near-deterministic but can miss if a provider changed their name. Flagged for review, not auto-fail.

Combined coverage: 98.9% of individual exclusions on the list. Only 865 records are uncoverable (businesses without individual names, or records missing DOB).

6. Why we removed PECOS from the gate

We initially evaluated PECOS as a potential gate input. PECOS tells us whether a provider is actively enrolled in Medicare, and CMS does revoke enrollment for serious reasons (felony convictions, license loss, billing abuse). On the surface, it looks like a safety signal.

Why it doesn't work as a gate: Absence from PECOS doesn't indicate a problem. Many legitimate independent practices don't bill Medicare: concierge, cash-pay, commercial-only. These are exactly the providers Meroka is trying to serve. Failing them on PECOS absence would knock out good providers for no reason.

There's also a data limitation. The public PECOS extract only contains currently enrolled providers. We can't distinguish "never enrolled" (fine) from "was enrolled but got revoked" (bad) without historical snapshots or the separate CMS revocation files.

What we kept:

  • A pecos_enrolled informational flag in the output. Does not affect pass/fail.
  • A cross-reference check: if a provider is LEIE-excluded but still shows as PECOS-enrolled, we flag that as a data integrity issue. It means CMS enrollment hasn't caught up to OIG's exclusion.

Where PECOS goes from here: It's a real input for Step 2 (specialty validation, enrollment history, practice affiliations) and Step 5b (Medicare billing patterns mapped to clinical bundles). PECOS earns its keep in quality, not in safety.

  • ✓ All 200 clean providers (sourced from PECOS) correctly passed
  • ✓ 5 name+DOB test cases (excluded individuals with no NPI) correctly caught (Tier 2)
  • ✓ A fabricated name correctly passed (no false positive)

8. More data we could pull but chose not to for the POC

NPPES (free, CMS): the full national registry of every active NPI in the US. We could do a bulk pre-screen of all providers in WV and MA instead of just a sample. Decided not to because the POC only needs to prove the logic works, not scan at scale. Eng handles that.

NPDB (restricted, paid): the federal database for malpractice settlements and adverse actions. The only place where malpractice payments are aggregated nationally. Out of scope because access requires registration as an eligible entity, which could take weeks to months. Three paths documented for a future sprint: CVO partnership ($1-5/NPI), direct registration as a health plan, or partner through an employer/TPA.

State medical boards (varies): license status and disciplinary actions. No single national database. 50 separate state systems. Plan is to test WV and MA directly, plus test FSMB Physician Data Center as the national production path.

12. State medical boards: WV done, MA pending

The safety gate extends beyond federal data (LEIE) to state-level enforcement actions. We started with WV and MA as POC states. WV had bulk downloads available; MA required a public records request.

13. WV: what we downloaded

WV Active MD Roster (wvbom.wv.gov/Rosters.asp): Excel file with all active MDs, updated monthly. 10,276 active license holders. Fields: first name, middle name, last name, suffix, license number, license expiration date.

WV Public Discipline Spreadsheet (wvbom.wv.gov/public/board-actions.asp): Excel file with all board actions going back to 1953. 1,440 records total, 1,367 for MDs (1,055 unique physicians).

NPPES NPI Registry API (npiregistry.cms.hhs.gov): Free, real-time lookup. Used to bridge WV roster names to NPI numbers since the roster has no NPI column.

14. WV: the NPI bridge problem

The WV roster has license numbers but no NPI. The NPPES API is the bridge: we query by (first name, last name, state=WV) and see how many results come back.

High
Name + state unique (69.9%): NPPES returns exactly 1 result for this name in WV. Deterministic match.
Medium
Fallback match (17.6%): no WV result, but name-only search returns 1 MD nationally. Covers cross-state providers.
None
Ambiguous or not found (12.5%): common names return multiple results. Can't pick one without DOB or license number as tiebreaker.
Overall: 87.5% NPI match rate. 273 of 312 providers resolved. The 12.5% gap is mostly common names.

16. WV: resolved by onboarding form

Three of the POC's matching limitations are solved by fields we already collect (or will collect) at provider registration:

✓ NPI ambiguity from name matching. We collect state license number at registration. License number matches deterministically against the roster and NPPES "Other Provider Identifier" field. Eliminates the 12.5% ambiguous rate.
✓ DOB as tiebreaker. We already collect DOB for the LEIE check. Can disambiguate any remaining fuzzy NPPES results. Available, just not wired up in this POC.
✓ Cross-state providers. License number match doesn't depend on NPPES address state. Deterministic regardless of where the provider lists their practice.
17. WV: remaining limitations

No action type in discipline file. The public spreadsheet has dates and names but not what happened (probation, suspension, revocation, fine). The board's website has this detail but it's not in the downloadable file. Either scrape the site or use FSMB.

Removed providers are invisible. Physicians whose licenses were fully revoked drop off the active roster. Our pipeline only catches disciplined providers who are still active. Galang (test case 2) illustrates this: he was disciplined and removed. Need historical roster snapshots (monthly diffs) or FSMB to catch these.

Each state is different. WV has bulk downloads. MA does not (requires a records request). This notebook is WV-specific. Each new state requires its own data acquisition and field mapping.

Step 4 is the only other step that requires provider-reported data. The schema is defined in the treasure map but hasn't been validated yet. Fields listed above are preliminary.

Fields we do NOT need from onboarding

These are things we get from the federal datasets directly. The provider doesn't need to tell us any of this:

  • × Exclusion status (that's what we're checking via LEIE)
  • × Medicare enrollment status (PECOS gives us this)
  • × Board certification (ABMS, Step 2)
  • × Specialty and taxonomy code (NPPES, Step 2)
  • × MIPS / clinical quality scores (CMS QPP, Step 5)
  • × Utilization and procedure volume (CMS, Step 5b)
  • × Patient reviews and ratings (Google, Healthgrades, Step 3)
