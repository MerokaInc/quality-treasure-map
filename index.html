Data source 2: OIG LEIE Done
2

OIG Exclusion List (LEIE)

List of Excluded Individuals/Entities · HHS Office of Inspector General
Free · Public

Every individual or entity excluded from participating in federal healthcare programs (Medicare, Medicaid, CHIP). If a provider is on this list, any entity that pays them for federally reimbursable services faces civil monetary penalties.

AccessFormatRefreshCost
exclusions.oig.hhs.govCSV bulk downloadMonthly full + supplementFree
What we found

The file has 82,749 banned providers. Our job is to check whether any provider joining our marketplace appears on this list. The problem: each provider on our marketplace has an NPI (their unique ID number), but only 10% of LEIE records have an NPI. The other 90% are from before 2007 when the NPI system started.

What those old records DO have: the person's last name, first name, and date of birth. "John Smith" alone would match too many people. But "John Smith born April 15, 1965" is almost certainly one specific person. That's why we built two matching tiers:

Tier 1
NPI match (high confidence): exact NPI lookup. Covers 8,306 exclusions (10%). No ambiguity, auto-fail.
Tier 2
Name + date of birth match (medium confidence): catches the 70,543 older exclusions that have no NPI (89%). Near-deterministic, but can miss if a provider changed their name. Flagged for review, not auto-fail.
Combined coverage: 98.9% of individual exclusions on the list. Only 865 records uncoverable (businesses without individual names, or missing DOB).

What this requires from the onboarding form: NPI alone gets Tier 1 only. To run both tiers, the provider onboarding profile must also collect legal first name, legal last name, and date of birth. Without DOB, we can only check against 10% of the exclusion list.

Validation: 10 NPI test cases caught, 5 name+DOB test cases caught, fabricated names correctly passed. Logic confirmed end-to-end.

PECOS — Provider Enrollment, Chain and Ownership System

CMS · Medicare enrollment registry · Not a gate input
Free · Public extract

CMS's enrollment system for providers who bill Medicare. We evaluated PECOS as a potential gate input and decided against it. It's kept as informational context for this step and as a real input for Steps 3 and 4.

AccessFormatRefreshCost
data.cms.govCSV bulk downloadMonthlyFree
Why we removed it from the gate

The file has 2,957,262 enrollment rows covering 2,542,195 unique providers. This is every provider who bills Medicare in the US. We initially considered using PECOS enrollment as a gate signal.

The problem: absence from PECOS doesn't indicate anything wrong. Plenty of good independent practices don't bill Medicare: concierge, cash-pay, commercial-only. Failing providers on PECOS absence would knock out the exact providers Meroka is trying to serve.

We also can't use the public PECOS extract to detect revocations. It only contains currently enrolled providers, so we can't distinguish "never enrolled" from "was enrolled, got revoked" without historical snapshots or the separate CMS revocation files.

What we kept: A pecos_enrolled informational flag (does not affect pass/fail), plus a cross-reference check: if a provider is LEIE-excluded but still shows as PECOS-enrolled, that's a data integrity flag suggesting CMS hasn't caught up to OIG's exclusion. PECOS remains a real input for Step 3 (Medicare billing patterns) and Step 4 (specialty validation, enrollment history).

S2

Government Quality Score (MIPS)

Has the government independently assessed this provider's clinical quality?
DONE
Objective
  • Pull the CMS MIPS/QPP final score for each NPI. This is the only place where a federal agency has independently evaluated a provider's clinical quality
  • Add indirect quality signal via hospital affiliation: CMS star ratings, HVBP scores, HCAHPS, infection/complication/readmission flags for the hospital where the provider practices
  • Produce a per-NPI output: MIPS score (0-100), hospital affiliation quality, and a confidence tier indicating how much the composite should trust this dimension
  • Strong ERISA evidentiary value. An employer can point to a government-issued quality assessment as part of their fiduciary due diligence
Business logic: confidence tiers
1

Tier 1: Government assessed (3.0%)

Has MIPS score. Full weight on this dimension.
  • CMS directly evaluated this provider's clinical quality across four categories (quality, cost, improvement activities, promoting interoperability)
  • MIPS score passes through as-is (0-100). Median MA score: 91.4. Scores skew high.
  • For low scores (below 50), the composite should cross-reference Step 1 (safety gate) and Step 5 (patient experience) before penalizing
  • 7,591 MA providers have a MIPS score (3.0% of provider universe, 11.1% of physicians)
2

Tier 2: Hospital signal (8.9%)

No MIPS, but has hospital affiliation quality data.
  • CMS assessed the hospital, not the provider directly. Indirect signal.
  • Hospital star rating (1-5), HVBP performance score, HCAHPS patient experience, infection/complication/readmission flags
  • Composite may give partial weight or use for cross-referencing only
  • 22,265 MA providers have hospital affiliation quality data (8.9%)
3

Tier 3: No government assessment (88.1%)

Redistribute weight to other dimensions.
  • No MIPS score and no hospital affiliation in CMS data
  • This is not a negative signal. It means the provider doesn't treat enough Medicare patients to be evaluated.
  • Most indie practices are commercially focused. The absence of government data is expected.
  • Other pipeline steps (utilization, credentials, patient experience) carry the signal for these providers
What was built
  • Notebook: step2_mips_quality_score.ipynb (28 cells). Loads 4 data sources, runs EDA, cleans, joins to per-NPI master table, applies confidence tier logic, runs 8 smoke tests
  • Output: step2_output_final.parquet — 21 columns, 250,974 MA NPIs. MIPS scores + hospital affiliation quality + confidence tier
  • Coverage: 11.9% of MA providers have a government quality signal (3.0% direct MIPS + 8.9% hospital affiliation). 88.1% have no government assessment.
  • Key finding: MIPS covers ~11% of MA physicians. Scores skew high (median 91.4). The structural ceiling of Medicare FFS data means most indie practices won't have a MIPS score. This dimension is high-signal when it exists but the composite must handle the 88% gap gracefully.
  • Composite guidance: documented in the notebook. MIPS score passes through at full weight (Tier 1). Hospital quality is indirect context (Tier 2). No data = redistribute weight (Tier 3). Never penalize a NULL.
Structural ceiling
All CMS data is Medicare fee-for-service. A physician whose patients are commercially insured will appear "dark" (Tier 3) in this data because of who they treat, not how well they treat them. We can't engineer around this, but we document it transparently. The composite treats Tier 3 as an information gap, not a quality gap.

S3

Utilization & Bundle Profiling

What does this provider actually do, how much, and are there cost red flags at the bundle level?
20% of composite
Objective
  • Pull per-NPI procedure-level data from Medicare and Medicaid: what CPT/HCPCS codes they billed, how many services, at what charge levels, for how many patients
  • Map those codes to clinical bundles by specialty (e.g., OB-GYN: maternity CPTs to Maternity bundle, surgical CPTs to GYN Surgery, screening CPTs to Preventive)
  • Score each provider against specialty peers and clinical standards. Flag cost or volume outliers
  • This is what enables bundle-level scoring with public data. Instead of one flat composite per NPI, you can slice the score by clinical service line
What's already built (quality-stream repo)
  • Data pipeline (notebook): Medicare (~2.9 GB CSV) + Medicaid T-MSIS (~2.7 GB parquet, 227M rows) joined at NPI + HCPCS level using DuckDB. Full EDA, schema harmonization, payer source labeling (Medicare Only / Medicaid Only / Both Payers)
  • Proof of correctness: reusable lookup_npi() function. Verified end-to-end with Dr. Brandon M. Lingenfelter (NPI 1790045821, OB-GYN, WV)
  • CPT quality intelligence mapping: 6 clinical domains, 20+ CPT codes, each with 3-4 quality formulas (red/yellow/green/blue flags). Domains: Prenatal & Global OB, Cervical Screening & Colposcopy, Surgical, Preventive & Well-Woman, Lab Utilization, E/M & Billing Integrity
  • Clinical standards mapped: ACOG Practice Bulletins (prenatal cadence, GDM screening at 24-28w, depression screening), HEDIS measure specs (PPC, CCS, CHL, PND-E, PDS-E with CPT value sets), Joint Commission Perinatal (PC-01, PC-02), ASCCP cervical screening guidelines, CMS E/M benchmarks
  • 7-step claims validation process: data integrity checks, prenatal care completeness (HEDIS PPC), C-section & delivery quality (NTSV rate), surgical quality (MIS rate), lab utilization outliers, E/M billing integrity, bundle score updates
  • Scoring framework UI: Next.js dashboard with 4 quality dimensions (Clinical Quality 40%, Service Accessibility 25%, Bedside Manner 20%, Comfort & Accessibility 15%). Each dimension has Reality/Standard/Delta/Context structure documented
  • Provider dashboard (proof of concept): full interactive dashboard for Dr. Lingenfelter showing credential flags, CPT quality intelligence by clinical domain, and scoring methodology
What still needs to get built
  • Compute actual dimension scores: the five-dimension scoring model (ACOG Concordance, Peer Comparison, Volume Adequacy, Billing Quality, Payer Diversity) is documented but not yet computed from real claims data. Need to wire the formulas to the joined dataset
  • Prioritize ACOG Concordance + Peer Comparison first: strongest clinical signals, simplest logic. ACOG is a binary check per category (did they bill at least one code in each of five preventive buckets?). Peer Comparison counts coverage of the 25 most common OB-GYN codes. Volume Adequacy follows once categories are mapped
  • Build peer benchmark distributions: for each specialty, compute peer cohort stats (p10, p25, median, p75, p90) for code coverage, volume ratios, and charge ratios from the 17,962 OB-GYN NPIs in the 2023 data
  • Dynamic provider lookup: currently hardcoded to one NPI. Need to generalize scoring to run across all NPIs in a specialty cohort and produce per-NPI output
  • Bundle taxonomy for additional specialties: OB-GYN proof of concept exists. Need to define CPT-to-bundle mappings for primary care, pediatrics, orthopedics
  • Output as notebook for eng handoff: consolidate the scoring pipeline into a standalone notebook that produces per-NPI, per-bundle scores as CSV/parquet. Format: npi, specialty, bundle, dimension_scores, composite_score, peer_flags, cost_flags
  • Connect to Step 2 (MIPS): for the cross-reference in Step 2's "low score" situation, the utilization profile provides context on whether a low MIPS score reflects admin burden vs. actual quality gaps
Claims-to-quality scoring framework (OB-GYN proof of concept)
What this is: A claims-only scoring model built from 2023 Medicare and Medicaid billing data. It turns billing patterns into a process-quality score: did this provider bill for the services a strong OB-GYN practice is expected to deliver, at volumes and billing patterns that look clinically credible?

Inputs: 2023 NPI + HCPCS/CPT rows from Medicare and Medicaid. Standards layer: ACOG, HEDIS/NCQA, ASCCP, CMS billing norms, peer OB-GYN distributions. Output: Five dimension scores plus one composite score from 0 to 100.

PECOS was evaluated as a gate input in Step 1 and scoped out. Here it provides Medicare participation context for bundle-level scoring: which providers bill Medicare, what specialties they enrolled under, and how that maps to utilization patterns.

1. Input layer: what a claims row gives us

Base unit: one provider, one code, one year. Enough to see service mix, utilization breadth, payment patterns, and cross-payer overlap. Not enough to reconstruct a specific patient journey.

  • Provider identity: NPI, name, specialty, city, state. Isolates OB-GYNs and benchmarks each against the peer set
  • Clinical signal: HCPCS or CPT code. Codes like Q0091, 96127, 59025, 87491 map to preventive or prenatal activities
  • Utilization: Services, claims, beneficiaries. Shows whether a code appears once, occasionally, or as routine practice
  • Money: Charges, allowed, paid. Charge-to-allowed ratios compared to OB-GYN peers for plausibility
  • Payer reach: Medicare only, Medicaid only, or both. Shows whether services are delivered across payer types
  • Important limit: No dates, diagnoses, or patient linkage. Model is strongest for process measures and practice patterns, not complications or timing
2. Transformation: claims-to-score pipeline
01 Normalize

Join Medicare and Medicaid at NPI + code level, deduplicate code variants, compute total service volume per code

02 Map codes to care

Group codes into clinical buckets: cervical screening, STI screening, prenatal monitoring, depression screening, prenatal lab work

03 Benchmark

Compare against board/society guidance where possible, and against 2023 OB-GYN peer distribution where standards aren't directly observable

04 Roll up

Convert benchmarked signals into five dimension scores, then combine into one composite for the lookup UI

Core idea: billed code pattern → clinical category → standard or peer benchmark → dimension score → composite score
3. The five scores we compute
1

ACOG Concordance

Standards-based · 30% of composite

Check whether the provider billed at least one code in each of five preventive categories derived from ACOG and HEDIS-aligned value sets.

  • Formula: (categories present / 5) x 100
  • Categories: cervical screening, STI screening, prenatal monitoring, depression screening, prenatal lab panel
  • Interpretation: breadth of guideline-concordant preventive activity. Does not ask whether every patient received the service, just whether the practice visibly performs it
2

Peer Comparison

Peer-normalized · 20% of composite

25-code reference set built from the most prevalent codes in the 2023 OB-GYN peer cohort of 17,962 NPIs. Measures how much of that common practice set a provider covers.

  • Formula: (typical OB-GYN codes billed / 25) x 100
  • Why it matters: rewards coverage of normal OB-GYN workflow, not just purity of code mix
  • Typical codes: 99213, 99395, G0101, Q0091, 76816
3

Volume Adequacy

Behavior check · 20% of composite

Tests whether detected categories show up at believable volume relative to a visit-volume proxy. A category that appears once in a very large practice should not score like a routine workflow.

  • Scoring: each detected category is scored ok or flag; score = percent marked ok
  • Current floors: cervical and prenatal labs ~5% of visits, STI ~3%, prenatal monitoring ~2%, depression ~1%
  • Neutral fallback: if no measurable categories are present, provider gets a neutral 50 instead of automatic fail
4

Billing Quality

Medicare pricing pattern · 15% of composite

Compares the provider's average charge-to-allowed ratio with the 2023 OB-GYN peer distribution. Integrity and plausibility check, not a clinical standard.

  • Metric: average charge / average allowed
  • Scoring bands: 100 inside peer p25-p75, 70 inside p10-p90, 40 outside that range
  • 2023 peer anchors: p10 1.45x | p25 1.80x | median 2.30x | p75 2.99x | p90 3.74x
5

Payer Diversity

Access proxy · 15% of composite

Measures how many of the provider's codes appear in both Medicare and Medicaid. Rough proxy for whether a service pattern is broad-based across the practice.

  • Metric: codes seen in both payers / total codes
  • Scaling: scores ramp from 0 to 100, cap around 20% both-payer overlap
  • 2023 peer anchor: mean both-payer overlap in OB-GYN peer set is ~10.1%
4. Composite rollup

The composite favors standards-based clinical breadth first, then routine peer coverage and volume credibility, then secondary billing and payer-breadth signals. If a provider has no Medicare charge data, billing drops out and remaining weights re-normalize.

0.30 × ACOG  +  0.20 × Peer  +  0.20 × Volume  +  0.15 × Billing  +  0.15 × Payer Diversity
5. What claims can and cannot tell us

Claims can support

  • Whether guideline-related services appear in the provider's billed mix
  • Whether those services appear at routine or suspiciously thin volume
  • Whether the code mix looks like a typical OB-GYN practice
  • Whether Medicare billing ratios look normal or extreme
  • Whether services appear across both Medicare and Medicaid

Claims cannot support

  • Whether the right patient got the right service at the right moment
  • Pregnancy timeline measures needing dates of service or gestational age
  • Complication, remission, symptom improvement, or neonatal outcomes
  • Diagnosis-specific appropriateness without ICD linkage
  • True care-journey completion from prenatal to postpartum
6. Where the benchmarks come from

Clinical guidance

  • ACOG: preventive care, prenatal workflow, depression screening expectations
  • HEDIS / NCQA: cervical cancer screening, chlamydia screening, postpartum and depression-related process concepts
  • ASCCP: cervical follow-up logic for future patient-level scoring

Claims-native benchmarks

  • 2023 OB-GYN peer set: 17,962 NPIs used to define typical-code coverage
  • Medicare ratio bands: peer percentiles used for billing-quality scoring
  • Payer overlap baseline: 10.1% mean both-payer overlap across the peer cohort
Bottom line: This score is best understood as claims-derived clinical completeness plus practice-pattern credibility. Strong enough to rank providers, surface care gaps like missing depression screening, and drive a UI. Not yet a definitive outcome score. That requires patient-linked claims with dates, diagnoses, and episode logic (Step 7).
Structural caveat

Both Medicare and Medicaid datasets are government-payer only. Providers whose patients are mostly commercially insured will still look thin. We can't engineer around this, but we can be transparent about it. Combining Medicare + Medicaid gives a much fuller picture, especially for specialties with heavy Medicaid populations (OB-GYN, pediatrics, primary care).

Step 1 Report: Safety Gate

Federal exclusion check + state board disciplinary check · March 2026
1. What we set out to do

Build the safety gate: check whether a provider should be allowed into the Meroka marketplace at all. Binary pass/fail. The gate checks two things: (1) is this provider on a federal exclusion list, and (2) does their state medical board have disciplinary actions on file. A hit on either one is a flag. No weighting, no partial credit.

We also evaluated PECOS (Medicare enrollment) as a potential gate input and decided against it. It's kept as informational context for downstream steps.

3. Federal check: LEIE exclusion matching

The LEIE is the federal government's list of every provider banned from Medicare, Medicaid, and CHIP. The problem: only 10.3% of records have a usable NPI. The rest are from before 2007. We solved this with two matching tiers:

Tier 1
Exact NPI match (high confidence). Covers 8,306 exclusions. Auto-fail in production.
Tier 2
Last name + first name + DOB match (medium confidence). Covers 70,543 additional exclusions. Flagged for review, not auto-fail.

Combined coverage: 98.9% of individual exclusions. Only 865 records uncoverable (businesses without individual names, or missing DOB).

4. State check: WV board disciplinary matching

State boards track license status, disciplinary actions, consent orders, and practice restrictions. We started with WV because it had bulk downloads available. The WV roster has license numbers but no NPI, so we bridge through the NPPES API by querying (first name, last name, state).

High
Name + state unique (69.9%): one result in NPPES for that name in WV. Deterministic.
Medium
Fallback match (17.6%): name-only search returns 1 MD nationally. Covers cross-state providers.
None
Ambiguous (12.5%): common names. Resolved in production by collecting state license number at onboarding.

Result: 292 active WV MDs flagged with disciplinary history. 87.5% NPI match rate on the POC sample (312 providers).

5. Why PECOS was removed from the gate

Absence from PECOS doesn't indicate a problem. Many legitimate independent practices don't bill Medicare: concierge, cash-pay, commercial-only. Failing them on PECOS absence would knock out the exact providers Meroka is trying to serve.

We kept a pecos_enrolled informational flag (does not affect pass/fail) plus a cross-reference check: if a provider is LEIE-excluded but still PECOS-enrolled, that's a data integrity flag. PECOS is a real input for Steps 3 and 4, not for safety.
