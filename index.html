Business logic
  • Query each NPI against NPDB, state medical boards, and the PECOS/OIG exclusion list
  • If any source returns an active sanction, unresolved adverse action, or exclusion → provider fails
  • Not a score. Binary check only. No weighting, no partial credit
  • A provider who fails is not listed and receives no composite quality score
  • Refresh cadence matters: Hecht v. Cigna settled for $5.7M because provider data went stale. We need monitoring, not just a one-time check
Data source 1: NPDB
1

National Practitioner Data Bank

Operated by HRSA · Federal adverse action repository
Restricted access

The government's central repository for adverse actions against healthcare providers. The only place where malpractice settlements are aggregated nationally. A provider could have paid out multiple claims across different states and nothing would show on any single state board. NPDB captures it all.

What gets reported: malpractice payments, adverse licensure actions (revocations, suspensions, restrictions), clinical privilege actions (hospitals restricting hospital privileges), DEA actions.

Continuous Query: enroll an NPI for 12 months at $2.50. Get the initial query immediately, then receive automatic alerts for any new reports filed during the year. This is the monitoring mechanism that satisfies our refresh obligation.
Out of scope for this sprint. NPDB access requires resolving a registration question that could take weeks to months. We will revisit in a future sprint. The three paths below are documented for when we return to it.

Data source 2: OIG LEIE
2

OIG Exclusion List (LEIE)

List of Excluded Individuals/Entities · HHS Office of Inspector General
Free · Public

Every individual or entity excluded from participating in federal healthcare programs (Medicare, Medicaid, CHIP). Reasons include fraud convictions, patient abuse, license revocation, and billing violations. Some exclusions are mandatory (conviction = automatic), others are permissive (OIG discretion).

Why it matters: If a provider is on this list, any entity that pays them for federally reimbursable services faces civil monetary penalties. An employer directing employees to an excluded provider has direct legal exposure.

Full CSV download at exclusions.oig.hhs.gov. Monthly supplement file for new additions only. NPI included for most records; name + address for older records. No registration, no API key.

Data source 3: PECOS
3

PECOS — Provider Enrollment, Chain and Ownership System

CMS · Medicare enrollment registry
Free · Public extract

CMS's enrollment system for providers who bill Medicare. Active enrollment in PECOS is a baseline signal that the provider is known to CMS and in good standing. A revocation is a serious flag — CMS revokes for felony convictions, license loss, OIG exclusion, or billing abuse.

What we check: enrollment status (active vs. inactive/revoked/deactivated), specialty alignment, practice location.

CMS publishes the Medicare Fee-for-Service Public Provider Enrollment file on data.cms.gov. Free bulk download, updated monthly. No registration required.

Data source 4: State medical boards
4

State Medical Boards

50 states + DC · License status & disciplinary actions
Varies by state

License status, disciplinary actions (suspensions, revocations, probation, reprimands), consent orders, and practice restrictions. No single national database equivalent to LEIE or PECOS. Each state runs its own board with its own data infrastructure.

TierStatesWhat's available
Good~15 statesBulk download or clean API (CA, TX, NY)
Okay~20 statesIndividual lookup pages, scrapeable with effort
Bad~15 statesPDF-only, manual lookup only

No standardized format. Many boards use license number rather than NPI, requiring a crosswalk.

FSMB Physician Data Center aggregates data from all 70+ medical and osteopathic boards in the US. License status, disciplinary history, board certifications. This is what hospitals and health plans use. One contract solves the 50-state problem. Paid enterprise service. DocInfo.org is FSMB's free public lookup (one provider at a time, not useful at scale).
This sprint: two parallel tests. (1) Pull directly from West Virginia and Massachusetts state boards to prove per-state logic. (2) Test FSMB Physician Data Center against the same two states to validate coverage and data quality. If FSMB matches, it becomes the production path for national expansion.
