Claims to Quality

How 2023 OB-GYN claims become quality scores.

This is a claims-only scoring model. It does not pretend to measure outcomes we cannot see. Instead, it turns Medicare and Medicaid billing patterns into a process-quality score: Did this provider bill for the services a strong OB-GYN practice is expected to deliver, at volumes and billing patterns that look clinically credible?

Inputs 2023 NPI + HCPCS/CPT rows from Medicare and Medicaid.
Standards Layer ACOG, HEDIS/NCQA, ASCCP, CMS billing norms, peer OB-GYN distributions.
Output Five dimension scores plus one composite score from 0 to 100.
Diagram View rewrites each score as a left-to-right flow chart.
1. Input Layer

What a claims row gives us

Our base unit is one provider, one code, one year. That is enough to see service mix, utilization breadth, payment patterns, and cross-payer overlap. It is not enough to reconstruct one specific patient journey end to end.

Provider Identity
NPI, name, specialty, city, state

Lets us isolate OB-GYNs and benchmark each provider against the rest of the OB-GYN peer set.

Clinical Signal
HCPCS or CPT code

This is the clinical breadcrumb. Codes such as Q0091, 96127, 59025, and 87491 map to preventive or prenatal activities.

Utilization
Services, claims, beneficiaries

Tells us whether a code appears once, occasionally, or as part of routine practice behavior.

Money
Charges, allowed, paid

Gives us a billing-pattern view. Charge-to-allowed ratios can be compared to OB-GYN peers for plausibility.

Payer Reach
Medicare only, Medicaid only, both payers

Shows whether the same services are delivered across payer types or only in one slice of the panel.

Important Limit
No dates, diagnoses, or patient linkage

So this model is strongest for process measures and practice patterns, not complications, timing, or patient-level appropriateness.

One row means: NPI + HCPCS code + 2023 totals. We aggregate thousands of these rows into provider-level quality signals.
2. Transformation

The claims-to-score pipeline

The workflow is simple: clean the claims, map them into clinical meaning, compare them to standards and peers, then roll them up into dimension scores.

01

Normalize

Join Medicare and Medicaid at the NPI + code level, deduplicate code variants, and compute total service volume per code.

02

Map Codes to Care

Group individual codes into clinically recognizable buckets such as cervical screening, STI screening, prenatal monitoring, depression screening, and prenatal lab work.

03

Benchmark

For each bucket, compare the provider against board and society guidance where possible, and against the 2023 OB-GYN peer distribution where standards are not directly observable.

04

Roll Up

Convert the benchmarked signals into five dimension scores, then combine them into one composite score for the lookup UI.

Core idea
billed code pattern -> clinical category -> standard or peer benchmark -> dimension score -> composite score
What this really measures
routine practice behavior, not direct patient outcomes
3. Current Scorecard

The five scores we compute today

These are the exact dimensions behind the OB-GYN lookup page. Some are standards-based. Some are peer-normalized heuristics. Together they answer one practical question: does this provider look like a clinically complete, routine, and credible OB-GYN practice?

ACOG Concordance

Standards Based

We check whether the provider billed at least one code in each of five preventive categories derived from ACOG and HEDIS-aligned value sets.

  • Formula: (categories present / 5) x 100
  • Categories: cervical screening, STI screening, prenatal monitoring, depression screening, prenatal lab panel
  • Interpretation: breadth of guideline-concordant preventive activity

Peer Comparison

Peer Normalized

We built a 25-code reference set from the most prevalent codes in the 2023 OB-GYN peer cohort of 17,962 NPIs. Then we measure how much of that common practice set a provider covers.

  • Formula: (typical OB-GYN codes billed / 25) x 100
  • Why this matters: it rewards coverage of normal OB-GYN workflow, not just purity of code mix
  • Typical examples: 99213, 99395, G0101, Q0091, 76816

Volume Adequacy

Behavior Check

Presence alone is weak. We also test whether detected categories show up at believable volume relative to a visit-volume proxy. A category that appears once in a very large practice should not score like a routine workflow.

  • Formula: each detected category is scored ok or flag; the score is the percent marked ok
  • Current floors: cervical and prenatal labs roughly 5 percent of visits, STI about 3 percent, prenatal monitoring about 2 percent, depression about 1 percent
  • Neutral fallback: if no measurable categories are present, the provider gets a neutral 50 instead of an automatic fail

Billing Quality

Medicare Pricing Pattern

For Medicare-coded services, we compare the provider's average charge-to-allowed ratio with the 2023 OB-GYN peer distribution. This is not a clinical standard, but it is a useful integrity and plausibility check.

  • Metric: average charge / average allowed
  • Scoring bands: 100 inside peer p25 to p75, 70 inside p10 to p90, 40 outside that range
  • 2023 peer anchors: p10 1.45x, p25 1.80x, median 2.30x, p75 2.99x, p90 3.74x

Payer Diversity

Access Proxy

We measure how many of the provider's codes appear in both Medicare and Medicaid, rather than only one payer. That is a rough proxy for whether a service pattern is broad-based across the practice instead of payer-specific.

  • Metric: codes seen in both payers / total codes
  • Scaling: scores ramp from 0 to 100 and hit the cap around 20 percent both-payer overlap
  • 2023 peer anchor: the mean both-payer overlap in the OB-GYN peer set is about 10.1%
4. Rollup

How the composite score is assembled

The composite score favors actual standards-based clinical breadth first, then routine peer coverage and volume credibility, then secondary billing and payer-breadth signals.

Composite formula

The lookup page computes a weighted average across the five dimensions. If a provider has no Medicare charge data, the billing dimension drops out and the remaining weights are re-normalized.

0.30 x ACOG + 0.20 x Peer + 0.20 x Volume + 0.15 x Billing + 0.15 x Payer Diversity

5. Boundaries

What claims can tell us, and what they cannot

This matters. The score is useful precisely because it stays within what aggregate claims can support. It is a process and pattern score, not a full patient-outcome score.

Claims can support

  • Whether guideline-related services appear in the provider's billed mix.
  • Whether those services appear at routine or suspiciously thin volume.
  • Whether the provider's code mix looks like a typical OB-GYN practice.
  • Whether Medicare billing ratios look normal or extreme.
  • Whether services appear across both Medicare and Medicaid populations.

Claims cannot support

  • Whether the right patient got the right service at the right moment.
  • Pregnancy timeline measures that need dates of service or gestational age.
  • Complication, remission, symptom improvement, or neonatal outcomes.
  • Diagnosis-specific appropriateness without ICD linkage.
  • True care-journey completion for one patient from prenatal to postpartum.
6. Standards Layer

Where the benchmarks come from

We are not inventing a score from thin air. The model anchors itself in public standards where possible, and uses peer normalization where claims data cannot directly express a formal quality measure.

Clinical guidance

  • ACOG: preventive care, prenatal workflow, depression screening expectations.
  • HEDIS / NCQA: cervical cancer screening, chlamydia screening, postpartum and depression-related process concepts.
  • ASCCP: cervical follow-up logic for future patient-level scoring.

Claims-native benchmarks

  • 2023 OB-GYN peer set: 17,962 NPIs used to define typical-code coverage.
  • Medicare ratio bands: peer percentiles used for billing-quality scoring.
  • Payer overlap baseline: 10.1 percent mean both-payer overlap across the peer cohort.
Bottom line: this score is best understood as claims-derived clinical completeness plus practice-pattern credibility. It is strong enough to rank providers, surface care gaps like missing depression screening, and drive a UI. It is not yet a definitive outcome score. To reach that level, we would need patient-linked claims with dates, diagnoses, and episode logic.
